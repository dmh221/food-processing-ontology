Analysis

Seven electrolyte drinks, scores 4 to 62. They all promise the same thing — hydration — but the ingredient lists range from salt water to a synthetic cocktail of artificial colors, dual NNS systems, and industrial emulsifiers.

Key Takeaways

  • LMNT (4) is the baseline: sparkling water, salt, citric acid, stevia. Seven ingredients, one Tier C hit. The electrolytes come from mineral salts, not industrial phosphates.
  • BODYARMOR (23) uses coconut water as its base but packs 25 g of cane sugar per bottle. The “natural” branding masks a sugar load that rivals Gatorade.
  • Prime Hydration (32) has zero sugar but 3 Tier A hits: sucralose, acesulfame K, and natural flavor. Dipotassium phosphate (Tier B) and gum arabic add further formulation layers. AFS = 29 drives most of the score.
  • Gatorade (34) is the original — sugar + dextrose for energy, Yellow 5 for color, gum arabic for mouthfeel. The 1965 formula, barely changed. MDS = 3 from dextrose; Yellow 5 is the only artificial color in this lineup until Pedialyte.
  • Liquid I.V. (49) is the surprise: marketed as premium hydration, it scores highest on MLS (13) because it’s a powder that concentrates sugar + dextrose + 500 mg sodium into one stick. Silicon dioxide (anti-caking) and four Tier B salts (two citrates, phosphate, silicon dioxide) push AFS.
  • Propel (52) is Gatorade’s zero-calorie sibling — same parent company, very different formula. Maltodextrin + modified food starch replace sugar (MDS = 8, highest here), while sucralose + acesulfame K provide sweetness. Calcium disodium EDTA (a chelating agent) is a rare Tier A ingredient that few consumers would recognize.
  • Pedialyte Sport (62) is the extreme: 4 Tier A hits including Blue 1 and artificial flavor. Designed for clinical rehydration, it uses dual NNS (sucralose + acesulfame K) and scores like a heavily processed food despite having only 30 calories.

The Hydration Trade-off

  • The scatter chart reveals two clusters: BODYARMOR and Gatorade deliver sodium through sugar-heavy formulas (21–25 g); LMNT, Liquid I.V., and Pedialyte deliver high sodium (490–500 mg) with minimal sugar. Processing fills the gap.
  • AFS dominates this category: every product except LMNT has AFS as its largest sub-score. Electrolyte drinks are essentially additive-delivery systems — phosphate salts, gum stabilizers, NNS, and colors.
  • Prime and Pedialyte both use sucralose + acesulfame K, but Pedialyte adds Blue 1 and artificial flavor — pushing AFS from 29 to 42 and composite from 32 to 62.
  • The “cleanest” high-sodium option is LMNT (500 mg, score 4). The next high-sodium option, Pedialyte (490 mg), scores 15× higher. Same electrolytes, radically different formulation.
